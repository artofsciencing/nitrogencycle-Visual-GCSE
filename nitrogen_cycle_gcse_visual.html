Why is Nitrogen so Important?

Foundation ★

Every living thing — plants, animals, bacteria — needs nitrogen to survive. It is a key ingredient in:

🧱 Amino acids 💪 Proteins 🧬 DNA 🌿 Chlorophyll

Proteins are used everywhere in living organisms: as muscle in animals, as enzymes controlling chemical reactions, and for cell growth and repair.

✅

Remember for the exam

Nitrogen is needed to make amino acids → proteins → enzymes and DNA. Without nitrogen, plants cannot grow and animals cannot repair tissue.

☁️

Nitrogen in the Atmosphere

Foundation ★

The air around us is 78% nitrogen gas (N₂). That means nearly 4 out of every 5 molecules of air is nitrogen!

However, this N₂ gas is extremely unreactive. The two nitrogen atoms are held together by a very strong triple covalent bond (N≡N). This makes it very hard for living things to use N₂ directly.

💡

The Big Problem

Although nitrogen is all around us, most plants and all animals cannot use N₂ gas directly. Nitrogen must first be converted into nitrate ions (NO₃⁻) or ammonia (NH₃) before plants can absorb it.

📌 The molecule you need to know
N≡N = Nitrogen gas (N₂) Triple bond — very hard to break!
🌱

Nitrogen Fixation by Bacteria

Core ★★

Nitrogen fixation is converting unreactive N₂ gas into nitrogen compounds plants can actually use (like nitrate ions).

This is done by special nitrogen-fixing bacteria found in two places:

🫘

1. Root nodule bacteria (in legumes)

Legumes — peas, beans, clover and lentils — have bumpy swellings on their roots called nodules. Bacteria inside these nodules convert N₂ from air into nitrates the plant can use. This is mutualism: the plant gives the bacteria sugars; the bacteria give the plant fixed nitrogen. Both win! 🤝

🌍

2. Free-living soil bacteria

Some bacteria live freely in the soil and also fix N₂. When they die, the nitrogen they stored is released into the soil as ammonium (NH₄⁺).

⚗️ Nitrogen Fixation — key equation
1 N₂ → NH₃ → NO₃⁻ via fixing bacteria
N₂ (gas) → Ammonia (NH₃) → Nitrate ion (NO₃⁻) → absorbed by plant roots
📝

Exam-favourite question

Examiners love asking: "Name two examples of leguminous plants" and "What are the swellings on legume roots called?"
Answer: peas / beans / clover / lentils · Root nodules

⚡

Lightning & Nitrogen Oxides

Higher ★★★

Lightning provides huge amounts of energy — enough to break the strong N≡N triple bond in nitrogen gas. This allows nitrogen and oxygen in the air to react together and form nitrogen oxides.

These nitrogen oxides dissolve in rainwater to form nitric acid, which then seeps into the soil as nitrates (NO₃⁻) — a form plants can absorb.

⚡ Lightning — Step-by-Step Equations
1 N₂(g) + O₂(g) → 2NO(g) nitrogen monoxide
2 2NO(g) + O₂(g) → 2NO₂(g) nitrogen dioxide
3 NO₂ + H₂O → NO₃⁻ nitrates in rain & soil
🚗

Car engines do the same thing!

Inside a car engine, temperatures are extremely high — so exactly the same reactions happen: N₂ + O₂ → NO → NO₂. This NO₂ is acidic and causes acid rain and air pollution. Modern cars have catalytic converters to remove it.

Feeding: Animals eat plants and get their nitrogen by digesting plant proteins. Enzymes break these into amino acids, which are absorbed.

Excretion: Animals cannot store excess protein — they break it down and excrete it as urea (in urine/faeces). This puts nitrogen back into the soil.

Decomposition: When organisms die, decomposers (bacteria and fungi) break down their proteins into amino acids. Then soil bacteria convert these into ammonium ions (NH₄⁺) — a process called putrefaction.

🍄 Decomposition — what to remember
1 Dead proteins → amino acids decomposers (bacteria/fungi)
2 Amino acids + urea → NH₄⁺ (ammonium) soil (putrefying) bacteria
🦠

Nitrification & Plant Uptake

Core ★★

Nitrifying bacteria in the soil convert ammonium ions (NH₄⁺) into nitrate ions (NO₃⁻). They gain energy from this oxidation reaction. This process is called nitrification.

Once in the soil as nitrate ions, plants can absorb them through their root hair cells dissolved in soil water. Plants then use the nitrate to make amino acids → proteins → growth!

⚗️ Nitrification Equation
NH₄⁺ → NO₂⁻ → NO₃⁻ nitrifying bacteria (aerobic)
Ammonium → Nitrite → Nitrate (absorbed by plant roots)
📝

Don't mix these up!

  • Nitrifying bacteria: NH₄⁺ → NO₃⁻ (in soil, need oxygen)
  • Nitrogen-fixing bacteria: N₂ → NO₃⁻ (in root nodules / soil)
  • Denitrifying bacteria: NO₃⁻ → N₂ (see next section!)

Plants suffering from a lack of nitrates show yellowing of leaves (chlorosis) and poor, stunted growth.

💨

Denitrification

Core ★★

Denitrifying bacteria do the opposite of nitrogen-fixing bacteria — they convert nitrate ions (NO₃⁻) back into nitrogen gas (N₂), which is released back into the atmosphere. This completes the nitrogen cycle.

These bacteria live in anaerobic conditions (no oxygen) — most commonly in waterlogged soils. They use nitrate instead of oxygen for their respiration.

⚗️ Denitrification Equation
NO₃⁻ (nitrates in soil) → N₂(g) returns to atmosphere
Denitrifying bacteria · Anaerobic (no oxygen) · Waterlogged soil
⚠️

Bad news for farmers!

Waterlogged fields encourage denitrifying bacteria to thrive. They rapidly turn expensive nitrate fertiliser back into useless N₂ gas before plants can absorb it. Good drainage prevents this.

📝

The perfect opposite pair

Nitrogen fixation: N₂ (air) → NO₃⁻ (soil)  ↕  Denitrification: NO₃⁻ (soil) → N₂ (air)
